The unit economics of call center AI have permanently repriced.

AI voice agent: $0.44 for a 4-minute call.
Human agent: $2.70 to $5.60 for the same call.

That gap doesn't close. It widens as AI scales.

National Insurance Corp ran the math in production. They went from 200 contact center agents to 60 specialized agents by automating 80% of inbound calls. Saved $9.78M annually. Payback period: 3.2 months.

Credit unions — notoriously risk-averse, compliance-heavy, slow on tech — are hitting 60% containment rates in four months. Granite Credit Union: 1,400 hours of manual work eliminated in the first quarter. Service 1st Federal Credit Union: call abandonment dropped from 25% to 1%.

When credit unions hit these numbers, that's not early adopter data. That's crossing a deployment reliability threshold.

The broader picture: AI voice agents generate $3.50 for every $1 invested. Year-three leaders reach 8x ROI. First-year return averages 41%.

Gartner projects $80B in contact center labor cost reduction in 2026. 91% of customer service leaders already face executive pressure to implement AI. 70% of support interactions are expected to be automated by end of 2027.

Stop modeling AI as "cost reduction."

Model it as a structural repricing of your entire call handling stack. The economics at $0.44 vs. $5.60 per call make the business case binary: move now or watch competitors lock in 12-18 month structural advantages while you're still in evaluation mode.

The CFO question isn't "can we afford AI?" anymore. It's "can we afford to keep paying $5.60 per call?"

The organizations deploying now aren't just cutting costs. They're building a cost structure their competitors can't match.
