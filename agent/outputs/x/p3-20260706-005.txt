Three years ago, 53% of customers said they were satisfied with AI-handled voice interactions.

Today: 72%.

And 89% say they prefer brands that offer AI-assisted support options.

That last number is the one executives should be reading.

Not "customers tolerate AI." It's "customers are starting to expect it."

The CSAT trajectory matters more than the current score. Voice AI satisfaction went from majority-skeptical to majority-positive in 36 months. It's still moving.

Here's what's driving the shift:

**Speed.** Human average handle time for routine calls: 4–8 minutes. AI average handle time for tier-1 workflows: 90 seconds. For customers with simple questions, that's not a tradeoff — it's better.

**Consistency.** The best human rep has a bad Tuesday. Voice AI doesn't. For standardized workflows — billing inquiries, order status, scheduling — AI delivers the same answer every time. Customers learn to trust that reliability.

**Availability.** 78% of contact center AI deployments now offer 24/7 coverage. Getting an accurate answer at 11pm without waiting on hold is its own form of satisfaction that CSAT surveys barely capture.

The market size numbers confirm direction: $22.5B in 2026, growing at 34.8% CAGR. Gartner projects $80B in global contact-center labor savings by end of 2026.

But CSAT trend is the leading indicator. Market size tells you how much is being spent. Customer preference tells you whether it's working.

When 89% of customers prefer brands with AI support options, companies not moving aren't just leaving money on the table — they're losing preference points to competitors who are.

The shift is behavioral, not just economic. Behavioral shifts compound.

ROI is a lagging indicator. Customer preference is where the story starts.
