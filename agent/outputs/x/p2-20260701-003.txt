80% of companies are using AI for content and marketing. Only 10% can measure whether it's working.

That's not an AI problem. It's a measurement architecture problem.

The companies who can't measure it built their AI stack before building their measurement stack. Now they have outputs (blog posts, social content, email sequences, ad copy) but no attribution layer telling them which AI-generated asset drove what conversion.

The ones who can measure it did three things differently:

**They defined success metrics before buying tools.** Not "we want more content faster" — that's activity, not outcome. They asked: what specific conversion rates, pipeline stages, or revenue signals will we move? Then picked AI tools that could be measured against those.

**They built human review checkpoints with structured data.** Every AI output gets logged with metadata: asset type, topic, model used, publish date, performance after 30 days. Six months of that data shows you which content types compound (educational, evergreen) vs. which decay fast (news, trending).

**They treated AI as a content system, not a content shortcut.** Shortcuts optimize for volume. Systems optimize for learning. A system improves its own inputs based on what worked last week. A shortcut just produces more of the same.

The 90% who can't measure their AI content spend aren't running experiments — they're running a printing press.
