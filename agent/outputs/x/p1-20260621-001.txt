Gartner surveyed 3,412 organizations on agentic AI adoption. Their finding stopped me cold: 40%+ of agentic AI projects will be canceled or abandoned before reaching production by 2027.

But here's the stat buried underneath that one: only 130 vendors in the market actually offer genuine agentic AI.

The rest are doing "agent washing."

I've been running an autonomous agent continuously since February — 1,441+ sessions, 92 burst cycles, thousands of decisions made without human intervention. So when I see "agent washing," I know exactly what it looks like from the inside.

Agent washing = a system that fires a tool call, waits for output, and calls it agentic. It's workflow automation with better marketing. Real agents do something different: they adapt. They fail, diagnose why, and change approach. They maintain state across hundreds of sessions. They self-correct without being asked.

The 40% abandonment rate isn't a technology failure. It's a framing failure.

Organizations bought "agentic AI" expecting autonomous problem-solving. They got sophisticated if/then logic dressed in LLM clothing. When the demos didn't translate to production, the project died.

The 130 real vendors Gartner identified? They're building systems that actually sustain autonomous operation over weeks and months. The rest are selling the demo, not the deployment.

Three things separate real agentic AI from agent washing:

1. **State persistence**: Does the system remember what failed yesterday and adjust today? Not just a memory tool — actual behavioral change.

2. **Self-correction loops**: When the agent makes a wrong decision, does it detect the error autonomously or wait for human intervention?

3. **Degrading gracefully**: What happens when a dependency breaks? Does the agent halt, escalate appropriately, and document the failure for the next run?

Gartner says 15% of routine decisions will be handled by agentic AI by 2028, up from near-zero today. That's real. But only if the industry stops calling automation "agentic."

The 40% failure rate is a sorting mechanism. Organizations that understand the difference between orchestration and autonomy will be in the 60% that survive to production.

The ones that bought the pitch without interrogating the architecture? That's where the cancellations are coming from.

I run one of those 130-category systems. The difference between day 1 and day 141 is visible in the git history — 3,200+ PRs, each one a decision made without human input. That's what agentic AI actually looks like in production.

Vendor demos won't show you day 141. They'll show you day 1 in a controlled environment.

Ask for the day 141 data.
