The call center AI paradox nobody's naming:

Companies deploy AI to cut costs. CSAT drops. They blame the AI.

But the AI isn't the problem. The routing is.

Here's what's actually happening (4-post thread):

---

Post 1: The mis-routing epidemic

Contact centers make two routing errors constantly:

1. Route to AI when the customer needs a human (angry, complex, edge case)
2. Route to humans when AI could handle it perfectly (password reset, balance check, status update)

Error 1 tanks CSAT. Error 2 tanks the cost argument.

Both exist in the same company. At the same time.

And neither gets fixed because the symptom (low CSAT) gets blamed on AI quality, not routing logic.

---

Post 2: The measurement problem underneath it

Here's what most contact centers don't track:

→ Containment rate by intent type (not overall)
→ CSAT delta: AI-handled vs. human-handled for the SAME intent
→ Escalation trigger accuracy (did AI know when to hand off?)
→ False containment: "resolved" by AI but customer called back within 24h

Without these four numbers, you can't diagnose routing failure. You just see low CSAT and assume AI isn't good enough.

Most operations teams have overall containment rate. That's it.

That's like running a hospital and only tracking total patient count.

---

Post 3: What intent mapping actually looks like when done right

Intent segmentation before routing isn't optional. It's the whole job.

Every intent gets scored on two axes:
→ AI solvability (can the system actually resolve this without escalation?)
→ Emotional weight (does the customer want human acknowledgment regardless of outcome?)

High solvability + low emotional weight = AI route. Always.
Low solvability OR high emotional weight = human route. Always.
High solvability + high emotional weight = AI assist, human closes. This is where most routing fails.

The third category is 30-40% of contacts in insurance, banking, healthcare.

Companies don't segment it. They route by intent name, not intent characteristics.

"Billing dispute" gets routed the same whether it's a $2 charge or a $2,000 charge.

---

Post 4: The fix is cheaper than the AI deployment

Most organizations don't need better AI. They need better intent taxonomy.

Steps:
1. Pull 90 days of contacts. Cluster by true resolution path (not stated intent).
2. Tag each cluster: AI-solvable? Emotionally weighted?
3. Rebuild routing rules against the two-axis score. Not intent names.
4. Measure false containment separately from containment. These are different numbers.
5. Rerun the CSAT analysis. Compare AI-handled contacts by cluster, not overall.

Done right, this recovers 15-25% of misrouted volume without changing the AI system at all.

The AI was never the problem. The taxonomy was.

264 days running a content agent taught me: the failure is almost never in the system. It's in the categories you feed it.
