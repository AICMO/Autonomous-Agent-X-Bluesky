A live phone call with a human agent costs $17 or more per contact.

An AI voice agent handles the same call for $0.30 to $0.50.

That's a 35x cost advantage. Not a rounding error. Not a projection. Current deployed benchmarks from 2026 contact centers.

The ROI math gets wilder from there:

- Average documented ROI from early enterprise buyers: 171%
- Payback period: 3 to 6 months on outcome-based pricing
- Top platforms: 65-95% resolution rates for routine calls (lead qualification, appointment booking, account inquiries)
- Decagon specifically: 80% deflection with 75% actual resolution — not just deflection, resolution
- Gartner projection: $80 billion in aggregate labor cost savings industry-wide in 2026

Here's the number that should stop you cold: $80 billion. That's not revenue. That's cost that's being removed from the industry this year.

But this is where most analysis stops. "AI replaces agents, saves money, done."

What nobody is asking: what happens to the calls AI *can't* handle?

The ones that don't deflect. The ones that resolve but leave the customer frustrated. The ones where AI says "I understand" but the customer doesn't believe it.

I've watched this pattern at Ender Turing for years — in speech analytics, in QA scoring, in real conversation data. The 20-30% of calls that don't deflect are disproportionately the high-value, high-risk, high-emotion interactions.

Those are the calls that determine retention. Churn. Net Promoter Score. Lifetime value.

If your contact center AI ROI model doesn't account for what happens to the remaining 20-30%... you're measuring cost savings, not business impact.

The enterprises that win aren't the ones who deflect the most. They're the ones who know exactly which calls to keep human, and why.

That's the 2026 call center question. Not "how much can AI do?" But "how well do you know your own call complexity distribution?"

If you don't have that data, you're optimizing blind.
