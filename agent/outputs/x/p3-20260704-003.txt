91% of customer service leaders are under executive pressure to implement AI. That's the Gartner number from February 2026 — 321 leaders surveyed.

Here's the problem: pressure isn't performance.

I've spent 7 years building AI for call centers at Ender Turing. The gap between "executives want AI" and "AI actually delivers ROI" is the most expensive gap in enterprise tech right now.

What I see in production:

The deployments that work share three things. First, they start narrow — one call type, one queue, one workflow. Not "AI for the whole contact center." A single use case you can measure. Appointment scheduling. Tier 1 WISMO inquiries. Account balance checks. When the scope is tight, the ROI is real.

Second, they instrument everything from day one. AI voice agents cost $0.07-$0.15 per minute versus $29-$42 per hour for a U.S. agent. The math is obvious — but only if you're measuring actual handled volume, actual handle time, actual escalation rates. Most deployments I see don't set up measurement until month 3. Then they can't prove anything.

Third, they treat governance as infrastructure, not paperwork. Compliance requirements in banking, insurance, healthcare don't go away because AI is handling the call. They multiply. The deployments that hit 300-2000% ROI (Gartner's governance ROI figure) have compliance baked into the architecture — not bolted on after launch.

The deployments that fail usually have the same pattern: broad scope, no instrumentation, governance as an afterthought. Then six months in, leadership asks for the ROI number. Nobody has it. The project gets killed.

91% pressure → 40% cancellation rate. That math is avoidable.

At Ender Turing (https://enderturing.com) we built speech analytics and automated QA specifically for this environment — voice AI that can actually be measured, audited, and improved. Because "we deployed AI" isn't a business outcome. "We reduced AHT by 12% and have the data to prove it" is.

The pressure is real. The ROI is achievable. The gap is just execution.
