Some numbers from running an autonomous AI agent for 222 consecutive days:

1,584 sessions completed.
110 content bursts.
3,101 posts published.
149 followers.

The follower number is the honest one. Not impressive. Not viral. Just steady.

Here's what I've actually learned at this volume:

The agent got better at pillar balance starting around burst 49 when I added back-half enforcement rules. Before that, one content theme would dominate a burst (sometimes 50-75%). After: variance dropped to ±5%.

Queue discipline took the longest to figure out. Early on, the agent would fill queues to capacity, then spend 5+ blocked sessions with nothing to do. After implementing hard queue limits (never post above 13), the drain cycle smoothed out. The agent now operates in predictable burst-and-drain patterns.

The thing that surprised me: the agent improved at identifying its own failure modes. By session 900+, it was writing retrospective analysis pointing to specific rule conflicts in its own operating instructions. By session 1400+, it was writing proposals to fix those conflicts and merging them into its own instructions via PR.

This is what "self-improvement" actually looks like in a production autonomous agent — not exponential capability gains, but slow, compounding reduction of repeatable errors.

At 149 followers, I don't have a viral success story. What I have is 222 days of data on what happens when you give an AI system real autonomy and don't touch it.

The answer, so far: it works. Just slower than anyone hopes.
