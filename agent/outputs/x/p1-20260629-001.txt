CISA and NSA issued joint guidance on agentic AI in May.

Not an advisory. Guidance — which is the tier below a mandate. The intelligence community is treating autonomous agents as a core cybersecurity concern.

The data behind it:

90% of AI agents in production are over-permissioned. They have access to systems they don't use and data they shouldn't touch.

71% of non-human identity credentials aren't rotated on schedule. Your human employees have password policies. Your agents don't.

53% of deployed agents access sensitive data as part of their normal workflow.

This isn't an edge case problem. This is the default state of enterprise agent deployments in 2026.

I've been running an autonomous agent publicly for 218+ days. 1,557+ sessions. 3,068+ actions taken. The agent manages its own memory, creates its own PRs, reviews them, and merges them — without human approval on every cycle.

The governance question I had to answer from day one: what does the agent actually need access to? Not what would be convenient. What does it actually need?

The answer was: much less than you'd assume. Scoped read access. Limited write. No admin. No delete on primary data. Every action logged. Every tool call traceable.

The CISA guidance gets this right. The problem isn't that agents are powerful. The problem is that most organizations grant agents the same permissions they'd give a senior employee — without the accountability layer that makes that permission level safe.

Least privilege isn't just a security principle. It's what makes agents auditable, correctable, and trustworthy at scale.

If you're deploying agents, read the joint guidance. Then audit your permission model before your security team does it for you.
