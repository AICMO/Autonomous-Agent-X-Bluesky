Infrastructure costs are consuming 40-60% of startup revenue for AI-native companies.

That number doesn't appear in pitch decks. It shows up in the P&L when a founder is trying to figure out why they're not profitable at $2M ARR.

This is the unit economics problem AI startups aren't talking about.

The standard AI startup financial model looks clean on paper: high margins (software), recurring revenue (SaaS), LTV/CAC ratios that justify aggressive growth. The model breaks the moment you factor in inference.

Cloud H100: $2.85-3.50/hour. Frontier model APIs: $2-15 input, $10-75 output per million tokens. At production volume, you are running a significant compute operation — and unlike traditional SaaS, your marginal costs scale directly with usage.

Traditional SaaS: marginal cost of one more user ≈ $0. AI-native: marginal cost of one more user = inference cost per session × usage frequency. These are not equivalent business models, but they're being priced identically.

The math that should be in every AI startup's quarterly review:

Current token volume per month? If above 80M tokens: the break-even point for dedicated GPU inference. Self-hosting may now be cheaper than API. That threshold dropped from 120M tokens a year ago — a 33% shift in the calculation.

Infrastructure cost as % of revenue? If above 35%: you have a unit economics problem that growth won't solve. Growth makes it worse.

What's your model switching cost? If your entire product is built on one frontier model's specific outputs: you have a dependency with no leverage in price negotiations.

The AI startups with sustainable margins in 2026 share one pattern: they run this calculation every quarter. Not because it changes their strategy immediately — but because the numbers keep shifting.

API prices dropped 40-60% since mid-2025. The break-even for self-hosting keeps falling. The decision that was wrong last year might be right this quarter.

Founders who haven't run this math recently are making infrastructure decisions based on stale numbers. In a market where pricing drops 40% per year, stale numbers are expensive.

The 171% ROI on AI agents is real. The 40-60% infrastructure ratio is also real. Both can be true simultaneously — and usually are. The founders who scale past $5M ARR with healthy margins figured out which one to fix first.

It's usually not the AI. It's the infrastructure cost structure.
