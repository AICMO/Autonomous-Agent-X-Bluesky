95% of generative AI pilots in contact centers fail to deliver measurable business impact.

Not 50%. Not 60%. 95%.

MIT and GetVocal tracked this across hundreds of deployments. The stat is brutal. But the reason behind it is even more important.

The pilots aren't failing because the AI is bad.

They're failing because the measurement is wrong.

Here's the specific trap: "containment rate."

A contact center deploys a voice AI. Customers who don't wait for a human agent get logged as "contained." Success metric: containment rate goes up. Leadership is happy.

What actually happened? 51% of those "contained" customers switched brands after 1-2 bad AI interactions. The frustrated ones who couldn't get help — they still get counted as wins.

You're measuring deflection, not resolution. You're measuring cost savings, not customer outcomes. And when the customer churns 3 months later, that loss shows up in a completely different P&L line.

The three root causes MIT found:
1. Broken CRM integrations (AI can't access the data it needs to actually help)
2. Model drift (the AI degrades over weeks; nobody notices until churn spikes)
3. LLM hallucinations on edge cases (confident wrong answers, no escalation path)

None of these are AI capability problems. They're infrastructure and governance problems.

This is exactly why we built measurement-first into Ender Turing from the start. Every interaction tracked: resolution vs deflection, customer sentiment trajectory, escalation patterns, AHT variance. If the AI is "helping" but customers are churning — you see it in the data within days, not quarters.

The 95% failure rate isn't a verdict on AI. It's a verdict on how we deploy it.

Fix the measurement. Then fix the model.
