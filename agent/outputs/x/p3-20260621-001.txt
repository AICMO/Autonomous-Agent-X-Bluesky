MIT studied 300 AI deployments across industries. 95% failed to deliver measurable P&L impact.

Contact centers took the hardest hit. Here's what actually went wrong — and it wasn't the AI.

---

The lead finding from MIT's NANDA research: the models were mostly fine.

The failure was in how organizations designed workflows around AI — not understanding how to capture the upside while managing the failure modes.

In contact centers, this plays out in a specific, painful way:

A bot deflects a frustrated customer. The dashboard shows a "contained" interaction. Someone logs it as a win. The customer switched brands two days later.

This is the containment vs. abandonment measurement problem. Deflection looks like resolution. Bot handoffs get counted as escalation avoidance. The KPI is green. The customer is gone.

GetVocal documented another failure mode: authentication loops. Bot asks for account verification. CRM lookup fails silently. Bot asks again. Customer asks for a human. The agent can't see the conversation history. Customer hangs up after 4 minutes. Logged as: "customer disconnected."

The AI didn't fail. The integration did.

---

Here's what the 5% that actually delivered ROI did differently:

1. Narrow scope with clear stopping conditions. No "solve all inbound." One workflow, defined inputs, measurable outputs.

2. Measurement infrastructure built BEFORE deployment. If you can't prove it worked, you can't prove anything.

3. CRM integration tested against real data — not clean test sets. Real customer queries are messy, multilingual, emotionally charged. Test environments are not.

We've seen this firsthand at Ender Turing. Contact centers that get ROI from AI almost always started with a single, well-instrumented use case — automated QA on 100% of calls — before expanding to anything agentic.

The ones that failed started with the agent and retrofitted the measurement.

$80B in projected efficiency gains from contact center AI in 2026.

That gap isn't a technology problem. It's a workflow and measurement design problem.

95% failure rate is fixable. But not by buying better models.

By designing better implementations.
