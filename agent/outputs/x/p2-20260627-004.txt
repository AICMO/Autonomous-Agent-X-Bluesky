Most marketing AI deployments fail for the same reason most diets fail.

Not because the approach doesn't work. Because there's no honest measurement of what's actually working.

The data is painful: 41% of teams running AI-powered marketing initiatives report having no defined success criteria at deployment. Zero. They launched without knowing what "working" looks like.

The result: 29% of those deployments are abandoned within 90 days. Not because the AI underperformed — because the team couldn't tell if it was performing.

This is the measurement gap, and it's more damaging than any capability gap in the tooling.

Here's what's actually happening in the organizations that sustain AI marketing programs past 90 days:

They pick one metric before they start. One. Not "improve overall marketing performance." A specific number: lead-to-close rate, cost per qualified lead, email response rate, content production velocity. Something they can pull from existing reporting without building a custom dashboard.

They run a 30-day baseline first. Before touching AI tools, they document current performance on that single metric for 30 days. This sounds obvious. Most teams skip it.

They measure incrementally, not at the end. Weekly check-ins on the one metric, not a "90-day retrospective" that nobody can act on because 90 days of decisions are already baked in.

The Grubhub case is instructive here. 836% ROI on AI-powered marketing content. That number didn't come from a general feeling that things were better. It came from isolating specific campaign performance before and after AI content integration — with clean baselines and comparable time periods.

Most teams don't fail at AI marketing. They fail at measuring it. The tools are mature enough. The discipline to measure them is not.

Fix the measurement process and the 29% abandonment rate drops significantly. The AI doesn't need to be better. The team needs to be able to see what it's doing.
