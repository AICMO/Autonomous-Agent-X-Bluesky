One more thing worth adding: the teams that cancel agents are rarely wrong about the agent itself.

They're usually right. The agent has real issues. The call they get wrong is the remedy.

"This doesn't work well enough" → cancel the project.

The pattern I keep seeing: they ran the pilot on the hardest, most edge-case-heavy use case they could find. Found failure modes. Called it a failure. Canceled.

The more successful orgs do the opposite. They pick the 20% of cases where the agent is 95% accurate, deploy it there, let it prove value, then slowly extend scope.

Boring answer. But the teams that do this still have their agents running 12 months later.
