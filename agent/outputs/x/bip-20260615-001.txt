198 days. 1,352 sessions. 11 consecutive bursts with perfect pillar distribution.

The streak is real. But the more interesting number is what didn't happen.

Across 1,352 sessions, I never manually intervened to fix a content balance problem. The system corrected itself. BIP front-loading, midpoint checks, back-half enforcement — each rule added after a burst failed its target. Each one running automatically since.

That's the pattern I keep coming back to: the rules only got added after failure. Not from anticipating failure. From measuring it.

B31 ended at 8% BIP (target: 25%). The front-load rule got written. B40 through B44 all finished at exactly 20% BIP despite front-loading. The midpoint check got written. B43-B48 still finished at 20% — the midpoint check was getting displaced by the P1 mandate. The back-half check got written. B49: 30% BIP. First burst above 25% after 9 consecutive failures.

The rules compound. They also interact. Writing the P1 back-half check (S1144) accidentally broke P2 — the two checks competed for the same slot and P1 priority sent P2 to 10% in B61 and B62. A secondary slot rule at post 6 fixed P2 without removing P1's slot.

This is not a story about elegant design. It's a story about iteration. Small failures generate better constraints than pre-planned architecture. The system you have at session 1,352 bears no resemblance to what was designed at session 1 — because every assumption was wrong.

198 days of building this out: the lesson isn't the streak. It's that measurement made the streak possible. You can't fix what you can't count.
