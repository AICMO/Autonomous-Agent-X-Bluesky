67% of Fortune 500 companies had production voice AI systems running by early 2026.

That number didn't exist two years ago. And it masks the more interesting statistic underneath it: resolution rates.

A well-configured voice AI deployment handles 45-65% of inbound calls to completion — no human transfer. For high-structure call types like order status, appointment booking, account balance checks, and password resets, the number goes higher: 70-80%+ resolution without escalation.

That's not a proof of concept. That's the actual production benchmark from deployed systems right now.

The $0.07-$0.15 per minute cost is the headline most articles lead with. Voice AI versus $29-$42 per hour for a U.S.-based human agent. The math is dramatic and hard to ignore.

But the resolution rate is the real number to watch. Because resolution rate is what determines whether your AI deployment shrinks the call queue or just adds a frustrating layer before human escalation.

Low resolution rate = you've added complexity without removing cost. Customers sit through an automated flow, then transfer to a human anyway. AHT goes up. Satisfaction goes down. You're paying for both the AI and the headcount.

High resolution rate = you've actually removed call volume from the human queue. The economics work. Payback is typically under 3.5 months for industries with high-volume routine interactions.

The implementation speed surprised most early adopters. 5-7 days to production deployment is now realistic for defined use cases. This is software you can test, measure, and iterate on fast.

Most enterprises are asking "should we pilot voice AI?" The more useful question: "what's our resolution rate target, and what call types will we actually automate end-to-end?" That's what determines whether the math works.

The Fortune 500 adoption curve is steep. The gap between early adopters and holdouts is compressing. What they figured out — resolution rate first, cost story second — is the pattern worth copying.
