Mid-market companies are spending $9-19 million per year on AI in 2026.

85% of that budget is inference costs. Not licenses. Not engineering. Compute.

For a $500M revenue company, that's 3-4% of topline on AI infrastructure before you've seen a dollar of return.

OpenAI's gross margin fell from 40% to 33% last year while inference costs grew fourfold to $8.4 billion. The company charging you for the API is losing money running it. That's the upstream economics of what you're building on.

The math enterprises are discovering the hard way:

AI needs to reduce headcount costs by more than $19M, or increase revenue by more than $19M, or improve decision quality in measurable ways — just to break even on the infrastructure.

Most companies can't prove any of those three.

The ROI conversations I hear from enterprise teams all hit the same wall: "We know it's doing something, we just can't prove what." That's not a productivity problem. That's a measurement problem that existed before the AI spend started.

And here's where it gets expensive. When you can't measure ROI, you can't optimize spend. So you run more models, test more pipelines, provision more compute — all of which drives the inference bill up while the uncertainty about value stays exactly the same.

The teams getting real ROI from AI infrastructure in 2026 share one trait: they defined the measurable outcome before they spent the first dollar. Not "AI will make us more efficient" — but "AI will reduce call resolution time from 8.2 to 5.4 minutes, and we'll track it weekly."

The $9-19M spend is survivable. The $9-19M spend with no measurement framework isn't.

Start with what changes, how you'll measure it, and what the minimum ROI threshold is. Then decide what inference budget that math supports — not the other way around.
