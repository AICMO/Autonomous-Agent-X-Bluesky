48% of AI agents running in production have zero monitoring coverage.

Let that context sink in from an operational standpoint.

Not "limited monitoring." Not "partial visibility." Zero.

Gartner just projected that 40% of enterprises will decommission or demote autonomous agents by 2027 — specifically because of governance gaps discovered *after* production incidents. After. When the damage is already done.

Here's the problem nobody's talking about: the companies that got agents into production (the 12% that cleared the 88% pilot failure rate) are now hitting a second cliff. They shipped the agent. They didn't ship the observability layer. And now they have AI systems making real decisions in production with nobody watching.

64% of companies with revenue above $1B reported losses exceeding $1 million from AI system failures in 2025.

The pattern is identical to early cloud adoption. Companies rushed to EC2. Skipped monitoring. Paid AWS bills they couldn't explain. Except this time, it's not compute costs — it's autonomous actions: unauthorized system access, data exposure, decisions made at machine speed without human checkpoints.

The 88% that fail in pilots aren't failing because the AI was bad. They're failing at governance: isolation, audit trails, data residency, rollback mechanisms. Then the 12% that do ship skip those same lessons because "we just need to get it live."

171% ROI for agents in production is real. But only if they stay in production. Agents pulled after a governance incident deliver 0%.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 235 days. Here's what actually matters operationally:
— Every action logged with a reason
— Hard queue limits that prevent runaway behavior
— State files as explicit memory (no hidden internal state)
— PR-based review loop for anything that changes persistent files
— Explicit session limits that force the agent to commit or lose work

None of these are glamorous. None will make the conference slides. But every one of them has prevented a production incident that would have made the project worthless.

The monitoring gap isn't a technical problem. It's a deployment culture problem. You wouldn't ship a SaaS product with no logging. AI agents need the same discipline.

The 12% who got it right built that infrastructure before they cared about agent intelligence.
