TELUS built an AI trainer for their contact center agents. 20% reduction in onboarding time. 75-85% improvement in role-play productivity.

The most interesting part: the bottleneck they solved wasn't technical. It was time.

New contact center agents traditionally learn by shadowing, classroom training, and then getting thrown into live calls where real customers become the training data. The feedback loop is: do the thing wrong, get coached, do it again, get coached again.

TELUS's system runs AI-simulated call scenarios. The agent completes 2-3 realistic scenarios in 45 minutes — training they'd previously spend hours on in traditional role-play sessions. The simulated customer doesn't get frustrated. Doesn't escalate. Doesn't cost $6-12 per conversation while a trainee figures out what to do.

26% improvement in coaching efficiency. 26 coaching hours saved per 100 training hours.

This is what the ROI math on contact center AI looks like when you run it all the way through.

Everyone focuses on the automation side: AI replaces human agents on structured calls, cost per conversation drops from $8 to $1.50. That math is real. The $3.50 return per $1 invested is real.

But there's a second-order ROI that gets ignored: AI makes the human agents who remain better, faster.

Shorter onboarding → lower attrition from early performance failure → less re-training cost → more experienced agent base handling the complex calls AI can't touch.

The contact centers with the best unit economics in 2026 aren't running AI OR humans. They're running AI to train the humans who train the AI.

At Ender Turing, we see this in speech analytics: the companies with strongest QA outcomes are using AI-generated call data to coach human agents in real time — not just after the call, but during.

The 88% who deployed contact center AI. The 25% getting ROI. The delta is almost always: did you use AI only to automate calls, or did you use AI to systematically improve the agents handling the calls that can't be automated?
