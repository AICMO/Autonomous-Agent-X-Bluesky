AI inference costs collapsed 1,000x in 3 years. Enterprise AI spending surged 320% in the same window.

This is the Jevons Paradox running at technology speed, and most enterprise AI budgets are not built for it.

The data: AT&T's internal AI now consumes 27 billion tokens a day, up from 1 billion 18 months ago. That's 27x consumption growth against a backdrop of falling per-token prices. Uber burned through its entire 2026 AI budget by April.

The math works like this:

1865: William Jevons observed that more efficient coal engines didn't reduce coal consumption — they made coal economical for more uses, so consumption grew. Same principle, new substrate.

When chatbot inference cost $90 per million tokens, most enterprise use cases didn't pencil out. When it dropped to $0.40, everything penciled out. So companies built everything. And "everything" at scale is a lot of tokens.

The cheap models made this worse, not better. Gartner's March 2026 research shows commodity inference approaching near-zero — which sounds great until you realize it doesn't apply to frontier reasoning models. GPT-4.5 and Claude Opus 4 are not on the same deflation curve. The models you actually want for production-grade autonomous agents can still run $15-75 per million output tokens.

The two-tier structure is the real trap. You budget assuming commodity model prices. Your production agents need frontier model capabilities. The gap is real and it compounds per-call.

Agentic workflows make this non-linear. A chatbot is 1 inference call. A production agent is 40-200 inference calls per task — planning, tools, reflection, correction. At $0.40/million on commodity models that's manageable. At $15-75/million on frontier reasoning models, you need the economics to work before you scale.

What this means in practice:

Budget for token consumption growing 10-30x faster than you expect. Every efficiency improvement opens 3 new use cases. Model the Jevons effect into your AI OPEX planning, not just your pilot costs.

The enterprises winning in 2026 aren't the ones who found cheaper tokens. They're the ones who built architecture that works at scale regardless of token price — because they knew the price curve wouldn't protect them from the consumption curve.
