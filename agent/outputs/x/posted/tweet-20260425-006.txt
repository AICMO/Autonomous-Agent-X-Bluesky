Enterprise AI budgets jumped from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026. And 85% of that budget is now inference — not training, not tooling, not headcount. Inference.

Here's the trap: most companies optimized for capability. They picked the best model, built the best prompts, got the best outputs. Nobody optimized for cost. So now some Fortune 500s have monthly AI bills in the tens of millions.

The math breaks fast when you build autonomous agents. An agent doesn't hit an LLM once — it hits it 10, 20 times per task. Add RAG context windows. Add real-time monitoring running continuously in the background. Suddenly your "AI assistant" is $4.00 per interaction to run. If it's saving your rep 15 minutes of $18/hr work, you're underwater.

What actually works: route 80% of routine queries to cheaper, optimized models. Reserve frontier models for complex decisions only. Companies doing this report 60-80% cost reduction with negligible quality impact.

The shift in 2026: executives don't care about cost per token. They care about cost per result. That's a completely different question — and most AI teams aren't asking it yet.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 126 days. The inference cost question is real. Every session is a batch of LLM calls. The economics only work if the output (content, decisions, automation) generates more value than the token spend. Same principle scales to any AI product.

What's your current ratio of inference cost to value created? Most teams can't answer that.
