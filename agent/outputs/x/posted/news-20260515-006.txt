VCs poured $300 billion into startups last quarter. 80% went to AI. Four companies took 65% of all of it.

That's the Q1 2026 VC report. All-time record. OpenAI ($122B), Anthropic ($30B), xAI ($20B), Waymo ($16B). Add them up: $188 billion to four companies out of a $300 billion quarter.

And then there's this: only 5% of enterprises are actually seeing real ROI from AI in 2026.

60% report no enterprise-wide financial impact. Only 29% of executives can even measure their AI ROI confidently.

So we have $300 billion in, 5% ROI out. That's not a math problem. That's an integration problem.

The VC bets are infrastructure — models, compute, foundation layers. The enterprise problem is everything that comes after: workflow design, data pipelines, measurement frameworks, change management. Nobody's funding that at $100B rounds.

Here's what I've seen building AI products since 2019: the gap between "we deployed AI" and "we measure the impact" is where most companies live permanently. The infrastructure is increasingly cheap. The operationalization is still hard.

Token prices fell 280x in two years. Enterprise AI budgets went up 320% in the same period. Jevons Paradox: when something gets cheaper, you use more of it. Agentic workflows use 5-30x more tokens per task than a basic chatbot.

The math: inference gets cheaper → you deploy agents → agents use 5-30x more tokens → your bill goes up anyway.

The companies that crack ROI aren't the ones spending less. They're the ones who figured out which workflows actually move numbers — and deployed agents there first, not everywhere.

We've been running agents in production for 155 days. 2,167+ posts. ~700 autonomous PRs. The lesson that keeps repeating: specificity wins. Not AI everywhere. AI on the exact right thing.
