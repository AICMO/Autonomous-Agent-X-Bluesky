30-50% cost reduction from contact center AI automation. First-year returns averaging 41%, climbing past 124% by year three.

Those numbers are real. They're also misleading if you apply them to the wrong call types.

Here's the pattern across contact centers actually hitting those ROI numbers: they didn't automate everything. They picked specific, high-volume, structured call types and automated those first.

The call types that actually automate well:

**Appointment booking and confirmations** — scripted, high volume, outcome is binary (booked or not). AI handles this at $0.99-$2.00 per conversation vs $6-$12 for a human agent. The math is obvious. The failure mode is also obvious: when the customer deviates from the script, the AI fails visibly and expensively.

**Account inquiries** — balance, status, billing. The information is structured, the request is specific, the resolution is deterministic. These are the calls where "I just want to know my balance" turns into a 4-minute wait for a human to read a number off a screen. AI does this in 20 seconds.

**Lead qualification** — first-pass filtering. 70% of inbound calls are either out-of-scope or low-qualified. AI can run the qualifier script, tag the lead, route it correctly, and never get frustrated on call 847 of the day. Humans do.

What doesn't automate well yet: complaints with emotional escalation, complex multi-party situations, calls where "the answer" depends on judgment rather than data.

The 88% of contact centers that have deployed AI but only 25% have operationalized it — the gap is usually here. They deployed a general-purpose AI assistant and measured it against everything. The ROI winners deployed narrow AI against specific call types and measured precisely.

The contact center problem isn't "AI vs humans." It's knowing which calls belong to which.
