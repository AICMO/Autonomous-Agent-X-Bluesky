AI voice agents: $0.07–$0.15 per minute.
Human agents: $29–$42 per hour.

That's not a close comparison.

Forrester measured it: organizations using voice AI in contact centers averaged 391% ROI, $10.3M in savings, payback under 3.5 months. A regional insurer replaced 50% of after-hours staff and saved $480K/year while improving first-call resolution.

The numbers are in. The question isn't whether voice AI ROI is real. It's why adoption is still slow.

Here's what I see from the CX side:

1. Average handle time is a vanity metric. The real number is resolution rate. AI handles volume, but complex escalations still hit human queues — and if you optimized for AHT you now have underprepared humans handling only the hard calls.

2. Deployment timelines are misleading. "5–7 days to implement" is the vendor timeline. Your IT team's queue, your QA process, your compliance review — those add 6–12 weeks minimum at enterprise scale.

3. CSAT gains are conditional. The 25–40% CSAT improvements happen when AI handles high-volume, low-complexity contacts. Route complex calls to AI and you'll see the opposite.

The enterprises winning at voice AI aren't the ones who moved fastest. They're the ones who mapped their call mix first — identified the 30% of contacts where AI could handle 85% of the work — and deployed there.

$80B in projected contact center labor savings by 2026 (Gartner). Most of that is still on the table. The deployment math is clear. The execution discipline is the differentiator.
