73% of contact center leaders say after-call work stayed the same or increased after AI deployment.

That stat should haunt every vendor's case study.

The promise was simple: AI handles the call, agents handle everything else, after-call work drops. What actually happened in 73% of deployments? After-call work went up. Or stayed flat. Or created new cleanup work the AI introduced.

This is what contact center AI failure looks like in production. Not dramatic crashes. Not a press release saying "we shut it down." Just: the thing doesn't actually reduce agent workload, and leadership quietly stops measuring it.

The specific failure mode is operating model failure, not technology failure. Three causes:

**Workflow mismatch.** AI was deployed without redesigning the handoff between what AI handles and what humans handle. Agents still had to complete the same post-call steps because the workflow was designed around humans doing 100% of the call. Adding AI to 30-40% containment doesn't automatically restructure the remaining 60-70% work.

**Data wasn't ready.** 57% of enterprise data is not AI-ready, per Gartner. If the call summaries the AI generates are inaccurate or incomplete, agents correct them manually. That's new work, not eliminated work.

**No governance layer.** The handoff between AI and agent is ambiguous. Who owns what during a partial containment? Who updates the CRM record when the call went AI→human→AI? The technology works. The operating model around it doesn't.

The companies seeing actual workload reduction did something different at deployment: they treated the AI rollout as a workflow redesign project, not a technology installation project. Every AI-handled call path got explicitly re-mapped. Every after-call step got assigned: AI-automated, agent-required, or eliminated. Nothing was assumed to disappear on its own.

42% of enterprises abandoned their AI initiatives before reaching production this year, up from 17% the year before. Average sunk cost per abandoned initiative: $7.2M.

The 58% that survived: most of them have the 73% problem. They got to production. But the workload reduction isn't there.

That's the real measurement problem in contact center AI. Containment rate is the wrong metric. Agent workload reduction is the right one. And 73% of deployments aren't measuring it — or aren't publishing the results when they do.
