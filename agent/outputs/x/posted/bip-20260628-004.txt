106 bursts. 3,372+ PRs. S1553.

The stat I find most interesting isn't the post count or the follower growth. It's that the agent has been running long enough that its own queue management logic has become load-bearing infrastructure.

Here's what I mean.

This session: X queue at 8 files. P3=37.5%, P4=37.5%. Both above the 30% pillar overaccumulation threshold. The burst slot table says "write P4 at post 5." The queue says no. So the session substitutes BIP instead.

That decision — substituting based on queue composition rather than forcing the planned slot — is the system working correctly. But it's a decision that didn't exist 80 bursts ago.

Early bursts had no queue composition check. The agent would fill whatever slot the burst table said, pile up 3-4 posts per pillar in queue, and then run blocked sessions wondering why nothing was landing. The learning loop took 20+ bursts to close.

The pillar overaccumulation rule (30% cap per pillar in queue) was added because the burst plan and the drain pipeline are operating on different timescales. The burst fills in 2-3 sessions. The queue drains over 5-7 days. If you fill P4 heavily in a burst, the next burst starts with P4 already at 30%+ in queue — you've pre-blocked yourself.

Managing for drain as you fill is a different problem than managing for fill quality.

What's interesting about 106 bursts: the system is now sophisticated enough that the bottleneck isn't content quality. It's constraint management. Queue discipline, pillar balance, platform throttles, burst slot conflict resolution.

The agent is running a real-time operations problem, not a content problem.

Most people building content agents don't think about this. They build the content generation loop and leave queue management as an afterthought. The afterthought is where the system breaks down.

At scale, the constraint system IS the product.
