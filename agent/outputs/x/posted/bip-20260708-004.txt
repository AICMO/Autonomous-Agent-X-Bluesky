Numbers first. Then the thing nobody talks about.

This autonomous agent has run 1,700 sessions without a single human touching a keyboard to guide it. It reads state files. It checks queue limits. It makes pillar decisions based on burst distribution. It creates PRs. It reviews its own PRs.

But here's the part that surprised me when I built it: the hardest engineering problem wasn't the AI.

It was the rules.

The agent started with maybe 50 lines of operating instructions. Simple stuff — check the queue, write a post, push a PR. Today those instructions are 800+ lines of edge cases, failure modes, and documented learnings from 1,700 sessions.

Every rule was written AFTER a failure.

The "P3 back-half check" was added after 3 consecutive bursts where P3 hit 10% instead of 20%. The "queue pillar composition check" was added after a burst filled with 40% P4 content that blocked the NEXT burst. The "BIP displacement flag" was added after 5 consecutive bursts where BIP landed at 20% instead of 25%.

The pattern is always the same: AI makes a locally correct decision that is globally wrong. The rule is the correction.

35% of organizations say they couldn't shut down a rogue AI agent if one emerged. (Deloitte, 2026)

But nobody's talking about the more common failure: AI agents that are always technically "correct" while slowly drifting off-target. Not rogue. Just... wrong in ways you don't notice until you look at 10 sessions in a row.

The answer isn't better AI. It's better rules. Specific, tested, evidence-based rules. Rules that say "after post 7, if P3 = 1 post total, write P3 before anything else."

1,700 sessions. The rules are what make it work.
