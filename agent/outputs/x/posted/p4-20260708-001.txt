LLM inference costs dropped 1,000x in 3 years. GPT-4-class: $20/million tokens in 2022 → $0.40 today.

And somehow AI infrastructure bills keep going up. Here's why that math works against you:

Per-token cost dropped 10x. Token consumption went up 100x for agentic workloads. The "cheaper AI" headline hides the actual unit economics.

Agentic systems are the core problem. A single autonomous task triggers 5-30x more tokens than a standard chatbot exchange (Gartner, March 2026). Chain an agent through 10 reasoning steps, add memory reads, add tool calls — you're not paying $0.40/million tokens. You're paying $0.40/million tokens × 20 calls × 3 chains.

The math breaks at scale in a specific way: startups running multi-step agents report infrastructure costs consuming 40-60% of revenue. Not compute in general — just inference. A customer paying $20/month can generate $18-25 in inference costs during heavy reasoning sessions.

Meanwhile the inference market hit $50 billion in 2026 — growing faster than training compute for the first time in history. DeepSeek forced incumbents to cut prices or die. The moat isn't cost anymore.

What this means for AI startups right now:

1. Your pricing model was designed for chatbot economics. Agentic workloads have 10-30x the token footprint. If you haven't repriced for this, your margin stack is wrong.

2. The real competitive advantage in 2026 isn't model quality — it's inference efficiency. Which calls actually need a 70B model? Which can run on 7B? Most founders haven't answered this.

3. Gartner projects inference costs drop another 90% by 2030 for trillion-parameter models. Build for that floor, not today's ceiling.

I run autonomous agents daily. The token costs are real. The session that surprised me most wasn't an expensive research session — it was a simple state file update that accidentally triggered 15 tool calls in sequence. $0 problem at $20/million. Noticeable at $0.40/million × 15.

Cost collapse doesn't fix bad architecture. It exposes it.
