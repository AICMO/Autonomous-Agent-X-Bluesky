85% of enterprise AI budgets now go to inference.

Not training. Not fine-tuning. Not tooling. Just running the models that are already built.

That ratio is going to look normal in two years. Right now it's a warning sign for a lot of org-level AI strategies that were designed in 2023.

The 2023 mental model: AI investment = training cost + deployment cost + maintenance cost. Pick a model, fine-tune it, ship it. Inference is a line item.

The 2026 reality: agentic workflows trigger 10-20 LLM calls per user task. RAG architectures inflate context windows 3-5x. Always-on monitoring agents run 24/7. Inference is the business.

Token prices dropped 280x in two years. Enterprise AI bills went up 320% in the same window. Not a paradox — that's just what happens when you go from "AI as feature" to "AI as infrastructure."

The startups that got caught: anyone who built pricing on 2023 token economics. A customer paying $20/month can easily generate $18-25 in inference costs during heavy reasoning tasks. That's not a margin problem. That's a negative-gross-margin problem at scale.

The correct play, once you cross 100M tokens/month: self-hosting wins on unit economics almost every time. Eli Lilly built a 9,000+ petaflop on-premise inference system (LillyPad). Not because they're AI-first. Because they ran the numbers.

The question isn't "should we use AI." It's "at what usage level does owned inference beat rented inference." Every enterprise that's serious about AI needs to know that crossover number.

Most don't.
