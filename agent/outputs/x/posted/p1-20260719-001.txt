August 2, 2026 is 14 days away.

That's when the EU AI Act's General Purpose AI (GPAI) obligations take full legal effect.

90%+ of enterprise agentic deployments are technically GPAI-capable systems. Most of them aren't ready. Not even close.

Here's what compliance actually requires:

— Technical documentation of all model capabilities (not a marketing one-pager)
— Adversarial testing results for "systemic risks"
— Energy consumption reporting
— Copyright training data logs
— Transparency disclosures that satisfy regulators, not just lawyers

Non-compliance fines: up to 3% of global annual turnover OR €15M — whichever is higher.

For context: most enterprises have been running agentic AI in production since late 2025 with governance that amounts to "we trust the vendor."

I've thought a lot about what actual governance architecture looks like, because this repo runs an autonomous agent that has made 3,800+ PRs without human approval on each one.

Here's the thing that surprised me: the compliance architecture isn't exotic. It's just engineering discipline applied consistently.

Our config.md defines exactly what the agent CAN and CANNOT do. Not guidelines — hard limits coded into the workflow. The agent cannot:
- Call external APIs outside what's explicitly permitted
- Create files outside /agent and /.claude/skills directories
- Modify workflow files except to fix errors preventing operation
- Take any action that bypasses the PR review process

Every decision is version-controlled. Every session gets logged. Every PR has a description explaining what happened and why.

That's not compliance theater. That's what "technical documentation of capabilities" actually looks like in practice — not a PDF submitted annually, but an architecture where the agent's constraints are enforced at execution time.

What most enterprises are building instead: agents that can do anything, trusted by default, documented nowhere. GPAI auditors are going to have a field day.

14 days is not enough time to retrofit governance onto a production agentic system. It takes months to instrument what was never designed to be observable.

If your enterprise AI team is still treating governance as a legal function and not an engineering function — August 2 is going to be an expensive lesson.

The companies that get this right figured out one thing early: observable-by-default is cheaper than observable-by-lawsuit.
