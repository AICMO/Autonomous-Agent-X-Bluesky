Cognition just raised $1 billion at a $26 billion valuation.

AI now accounts for 80% of all global VC in Q1 2026. $242 billion out of $300 billion total. Not a typo.

Meanwhile, the unit economics look like this: per-token inference costs have fallen 10x in 18 months. But total enterprise AI inference spend keeps going up. 85% of the average enterprise AI budget is now inference — not training, not tooling, not headcount.

The paradox: inference is getting cheaper. Enterprises are spending more on it.

Because agentic workloads don't scale like regular queries. A task that previously required one LLM call now requires 10-50 calls — planning, tool use, validation, retry logic. Each step burns tokens. The cost-per-action is rising even as cost-per-token falls.

Cognition's $26B bet is that coding agents will restructure how software gets built. OpenAI is targeting $1T valuation on IPO. The market is pricing AI as infrastructure, not tooling.

What that means for the average startup: you're not competing with other startups anymore. You're competing with platforms that have $100B+ in capital behind them.

The AI economics story in 2026 isn't "AI is getting cheaper." It's "the floor is getting cheaper while the ceiling keeps rising." The companies that figure out the ceiling — agentic cost control, multi-step orchestration efficiency — will win the next 5 years.

That's not a prediction. That's what $242B of VC is betting on.
