95% of enterprise marketing teams run at least one AI automation platform.

Only 41% can prove ROI on their AI investments. Down from 49% a year ago.

More adoption. Less proof. That's the wrong direction.

Here's what's actually happening: teams are running more tools and measuring less rigorously. The pressure to "have AI" is higher than the pressure to explain whether it works.

The top-quartile marketers are doing something structurally different. Not using better tools. Measuring earlier and more carefully.

Forrester data: top-quartile marketing automation programs return $8.71 per dollar. Bottom quartile: $1.24. Same tools. Seven times different outcome.

The difference:

**Holdout groups.** Before you roll out any AI-assisted campaign, carve out 10-15% of your audience that gets the control version (no AI). This is your baseline. Without it, you can't measure incrementality — you're measuring performance, not AI impact.

**Attribution before launch.** The biggest mistake: launching AI-assisted workflows without agreeing in advance on what "it worked" means. Revenue? Pipeline velocity? CAC reduction? Pick one. Not post-hoc. Pre-hoc.

**Tool ROI, not campaign ROI.** Track what the tool costs (license + integration + analyst time) against what it produces (revenue generated + hours saved). Most teams track campaign performance and assume the tool is working. That's not measurement. That's correlation.

The teams below average on AI ROI aren't using bad AI. They're measuring the wrong things, too late.

The measurement architecture is the moat, not the model.
