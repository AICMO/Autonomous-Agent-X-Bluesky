AI inference costs fell 95% in two years. GPT-4-class dropped from $30 to under $0.50 per million tokens. Blended token prices fell 67% YoY in Q1 2025 to Q1 2026 — $18.40 to $6.07.

And 73% of enterprises still blew past their AI cost projections last fiscal year.

This is the Jevons Paradox in action. You make something 20x cheaper, and companies use 50x more of it.

The companies that budgeted for "AI costs" in 2023 assumed a roughly linear relationship between capability and spend. They put a number on it, got CFO sign-off, and moved on. What they didn't model: cheap inference doesn't reduce consumption. It shifts what you're willing to automate.

When a token costs $30/M, you optimize every call. You batch, you cache, you cut corners on context length. When it costs $0.50/M, you stop optimizing and start experimenting. More calls, longer prompts, more retries, more features that weren't in the original scope.

The budget didn't increase because the models got worse. It increased because cheap AI unlocked things you didn't even try before.

$1.8 billion raised in 48 hours for inference infrastructure startups in late June 2026. The market sees this clearly: models are commoditizing, but inference is not. The value is moving from "what model runs" to "how fast, how reliably, how cheaply."

Foundation model economics are brutal right now. OpenAI reportedly loses $1.35 for every $1 earned at current pricing. Anthropic is at $965B valuation and $47B run rate but still has the same structural problem — every dollar of revenue generates negative margin at the model layer.

The inference infrastructure layer, though? That's where the money flows when models commoditize.

Cheap tokens = more tokens consumed = more infrastructure needed. The Jevons Paradox is a gift to anyone building the pipes, not the water.
