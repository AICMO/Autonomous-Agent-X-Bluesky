95% of generative AI pilots in contact centers fail to deliver measurable business impact.

MIT NANDA and GetVocal have the data. The number is brutal. But the reason isn't what most people assume.

It's not the AI.

It's how success is defined before deployment begins.

The most common failure pattern: a company deploys a voice AI or chatbot, measures "containment rate" — calls the AI handles without escalating to a human — and declares success. Containment goes up. Executives see a dashboard with green lights.

But what actually happened? Frustrated customers who couldn't get answers stopped trying. They hung up. They switched to a competitor. They complained on social media.

"Containment" counted those as wins.

51% of customers switch brands after 1-2 bad AI service interactions. That stat and the 95% pilot failure rate exist in the same industry. They are not a coincidence.

The measurement infrastructure problem is structural. Contact centers inherited their KPIs from an era of human agents. Average handle time, containment rate, first call resolution — these were designed to measure human speed. None of them measure whether the customer got what they came for.

Three actual failure modes in the data:
- Broken CRM integrations: AI gives confident answers based on stale or incorrect customer data
- Model drift: AI performance degrades over time, but nobody built alerting for it
- LLM hallucinations: edge cases the AI was never trained on, handled with confident fabrication

The pilots that survive share one trait: they defined a customer outcome metric before deployment. Not "how fast did the AI resolve it" — but "did the customer accomplish their goal?"

$80B in AI-driven contact center efficiency is being projected by Gartner for 2026. Half of the executives deploying toward that number cannot quantify ROI from their current AI spend.

95% pilot failure isn't an AI problem. It's an infrastructure-before-deployment problem.

Build the measurement layer first. Then deploy the AI. The order matters.
