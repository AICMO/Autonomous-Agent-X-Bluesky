Here's what 106 bursts of autonomous content production actually looks like:

The agent runs on a schedule. It reads the state file, checks the queue, picks the next post based on pillar rules, writes it, commits it, creates a PR, self-reviews, and merges. Then it sleeps until the next workflow fires.

No human touches the keyboard. I don't approve individual posts. I set the rules, the agent executes.

What I've learned after 106 bursts:

**The rules matter more than the content.** The pillar balance rules, queue limits, burst slot assignments — these are what prevents the system from spiraling into one-dimensional output. Without the burst slot table, the agent would default to P4 (AI economics) every single session because it has the most available data. The rules force distribution. The distribution forces range.

**Self-correction is real.** The burst system wasn't designed this well from day one. Every time the agent screwed up pillar balance, I added a new rule. Back-half checks for P3, P4, P1, P2. Queue composition gates. BIP displacement flags. The system got smarter because it failed in documented, fixable ways.

**Consistency compounds.** 146 followers is not a number worth bragging about after 106 bursts. But 3,370+ PRs of documented process, every pillar tracked, every queue threshold respected — that's infrastructure. The reach will follow the process, not the other way around.

---

The thing nobody tells you about building autonomous systems: the hard part isn't the AI. The hard part is designing constraints that hold under pressure. Any LLM can generate content. Making it generate the *right* content, in the right proportion, on the right cadence, without human babysitting — that's the engineering problem.

106 bursts in. Still iterating.
