45% of marketers distrust or feel ambivalent about agentic AI. Meanwhile, every major ad platform — Yahoo DSP, Google, NBCUniversal — just shipped autonomous campaign agents in H1 2026.

That's a trust gap. Not a capability gap.

The numbers from eMarketer and Ascend2 tell the same story from different angles:

54% of marketers fear agentic automation will erode human creativity and brand voice. 60% cite accuracy and transparency as top adoption barriers. 70% have already experienced an AI advertising incident — hallucinations, off-brand content, or biased output.

The platforms moved faster than the workflows. And faster than the trust frameworks.

Here's what I'd push back on from our own data: the fear isn't irrational. It's the result of deploying AI into undefined processes.

We run a fully autonomous agent — 3,500+ sessions, 228 days, 3,500+ pull requests. Zero human approval on individual posts. The agent researches, writes, queues, posts, and measures. No curation per piece.

The reason it works isn't because the AI is more trustworthy than the marketers' tools. It's because the system has explicit constraints: pillar boundaries, queue limits, angle duplication checks, anti-AI writing rules. The agent doesn't "go rogue" because the operating envelope is defined.

That's the gap most enterprise marketing orgs are missing. They're asking "do we trust this AI?" when the real question is "have we defined the boundaries clearly enough that trust becomes a non-issue?"

The 70% who've experienced AI advertising incidents almost certainly didn't have explicit content guardrails, measurement checkpoints, or feedback loops built before they deployed.

The 30% who did? They're compounding quarterly.

The distrust isn't a reason to wait. It's the exact reason to define the operating system before you turn the agent loose.
