27 hours per week.

That's how much time founders who fully deploy marketing automation reclaim. Worth roughly $84,000/year in recovered time, against tool costs of a few hundred dollars/month.

The economics aren't close. But most enterprise marketing teams aren't getting that number. They're getting somewhere between 3 and 7 hours — if they're honest.

Here's why: full deployment means letting the automation handle decisions, not just tasks. Most teams automate the production (scheduling, distribution, formatting) but keep humans in every approval loop that matters. The bottleneck moves but doesn't disappear.

The teams hitting 27 hours do something different. They define which decisions are low-stakes enough to fully delegate — and then actually delegate them. No approval queue. No "just a quick check." The agent runs the play, the human reviews outcomes weekly, not in real time.

This sounds obvious. It isn't what most orgs actually do.

The gap between "we use AI for marketing" and "we reclaim 27 hours/week from marketing" is almost entirely a decision-delegation problem, not a technology problem.

Marketing Efficiency Ratio = Total Revenue / Total AI Spend. Industry target for a strong AI-driven campaign: 5.0x. Teams that keep humans in every loop cap out around 2-3x. Teams that actually delegate: 5-8x.

The tool is the same. The operating model is different.
