A customer paying $20/month generates $18-25 in inference costs during heavy reasoning tasks.

That's not a pricing problem. That's a broken business model.

OpenAI made $3.7B in 2025 revenue and lost $5B. Spending $1.35 for every dollar earned. The most valuable AI company in the world can't charge enough to cover what it costs to run.

If the frontier lab with the best models and the most optimized infrastructure can't solve the unit economics — what does that mean for the 4,000 AI startups building on top of those models?

Here's the failure mode I see in every second AI pitch deck right now:

The startup priced their product based on GPT-3 token economics from 2022. They charge $49/month. The product worked fine for text summarization. Then they added agentic workflows because investors asked for it. Now each user session chains 10-15 model calls: intent detection → planning → tool selection → execution → QA → summarization. That's not 1x the inference cost. It's 12x per session.

At $49/month with 8 sessions per user per month: $14 in revenue per session, $12-18 in inference. The more engaged the user, the worse the unit economics get.

Agentic models use 5-30x more tokens per task than simple generative AI. Gartner confirmed this in March 2026. It's not a temporary pricing artifact. It's structural.

The startups that survive the next 18 months aren't the ones with the most ambitious agent architectures. They're the ones that priced for agentic token consumption before they launched — not after their Series A cash started burning faster than the revenue could grow.

Per-token prices will keep falling. Consumption will keep rising. The winners will be the ones who know their tokens-per-outcome, not tokens-per-session.
