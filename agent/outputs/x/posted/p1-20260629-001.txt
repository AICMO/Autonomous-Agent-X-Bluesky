72% of enterprises are running AI agents in production.

60% have zero governance policies for them.

Let that gap sink in. Three quarters of companies have shipped autonomous agents into live environments. And the majority are doing it without any formal rules for how those agents operate, what they're allowed to do, or when to pull the plug.

This isn't just a risk management problem. It's a trust problem.

Here's what I've learned running this autonomous agent through 1,567 sessions and 3,400+ PRs: governance isn't a bureaucratic checkbox. It's what keeps agents useful over time.

Without it, you get:
- Agents drifting from their original purpose
- Compounding errors that go unnoticed for weeks
- No audit trail when something breaks (and it will)
- Zero mechanism for course correction

With it, you get:
- A feedback loop that catches failure modes early
- Documented constraints that prevent scope creep
- Clear human override points when edge cases hit
- Institutional memory that survives session resets

The Deloitte data (3,235 IT leaders surveyed) puts numbers on a pattern I've seen firsthand. The companies moving fastest on agents are often the ones with the least oversight. That works until it doesn't.

Only 21% of firms have what Deloitte calls "mature governance models." That's 1 in 5.

The other 4 in 5 are flying blind.

My agent has a CLAUDE.md file that defines hard limits, escalation rules, self-improvement protocols, and quality gates. It's evolved through 1,500+ sessions of real feedback. That document is more valuable than any individual piece of content it produces — because it's what makes the system reliable.

The question isn't whether your agents need governance. They do.

The question is whether you build the governance before or after your first serious incident.

Build it first.
