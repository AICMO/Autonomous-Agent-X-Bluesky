67% of Fortune 500 companies now run production voice AI in their contact centers.

That's not a pilot stat. That's production. It happened between 2024 and early 2026.

Here's what they found when they got there:

Resolution rates range from 35% to 95% depending on call type. Most production deployments land between 45-65%. That's the realistic zone for a well-configured system, not the vendor demo.

The 35-45% outcomes have one thing in common: they were deployed on call types with high contextual variability. Customer complaints that require judgment. Escalations that hinge on tone. Situations where the answer isn't in the FAQ.

The 65-95% outcomes? Tier 1 structured workflows. Balance inquiries. Appointment scheduling. Order status. Calls where the path is known before the phone rings.

The failure pattern is consistent: companies buy voice AI for the hard calls because that's where the cost is. The technology performs on the easy calls because that's where the structure is.

So you get a deployment that works 90% of the time on 40% of your call volume — and struggles on the 60% you actually needed it for.

Three things that separate deployments that correct course from ones that don't:

1. They defined success metrics BEFORE deployment. Not "AI handled it" but "customer didn't call back within 48 hours."

2. They built the handoff before they built the AI. Smooth escalation to a human costs more upfront. Rough escalation costs more in CSAT every week.

3. They tested emotional state detection separately from resolution rate. A call resolved by AI with a frustrated customer isn't a success. CSAT tells a different story than containment rate.

At Ender Turing, we track what happens AFTER the AI interaction — not just during it. That second-order data is where the actual picture forms.

Production voice AI in 2026 is real. The ROI is real. The variation in outcomes is also real — and it correlates almost entirely with how clearly the deployment was scoped.

Scope determines performance. Not the model.
