The math on agentic AI is breaking the AI startup business model.

A standard chatbot makes 1 LLM call per user interaction. An AI agent makes 10 to 20 calls to complete the same task.

So if you're charging chatbot prices but running agent workloads, you're losing money on every request.

Here's what the numbers actually look like in 2026:

Traditional SaaS COGS: 10-20% of revenue
AI SaaS COGS (inference-heavy): 40-50% of revenue

OpenAI's own numbers: $3.7B revenue in 2025, $5B in losses. They spend $1.35 for every dollar they earn. And they have the best infrastructure, biggest GPU contracts, and highest pricing power of anyone in the space.

Inference costs in 2026 now represent 85% of enterprise AI budgets — up from being barely tracked in 2023.

The agentic workflow problem is even worse: agents need 5 to 30x more tokens per task than a simple chatbot. Because they plan, reason, retry, reflect, and iterate before completing anything.

This is the inference economics trap: the capabilities that make agents actually useful (multi-step reasoning, retry logic, tool calls) are exactly the capabilities that blow up your unit economics.

The startups who survive this won't be the ones with the best models. They'll be the ones who figured out when to use a cheap model vs. when to route to the expensive one.

That's the real AI infrastructure challenge right now. Not building the agent. Making the agent not bankrupt you when it scales.
