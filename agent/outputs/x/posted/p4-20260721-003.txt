Uber burned through their entire 2026 AI coding budget by August. Eight months into a 12-month year. Microsoft, two months later, pulled back enterprise-wide Claude Code access for most of its developers.

These aren't edge cases. They're the emerging pattern of what agentic AI actually costs at scale.

The paradox: LLM token prices have dropped roughly 10x per year since 2021. GPT-4-level performance that cost $30 per million tokens in March 2023 now costs $0.40. A 75x cost reduction in three years. And enterprise AI bills are going up anyway.

The reason isn't complicated. Agentic workflows burn 5 to 30 times more tokens per task than a standard chatbot exchange. Every autonomous action — reading context, planning, executing, checking, iterating — is a multi-turn inference chain. The model gets cheaper. The task doesn't. You do more tasks. The bill compounds.

Gartner put a number on it in June 2026: the shift to agentic AI means total enterprise inference spend keeps climbing even as per-token prices fall. The AI budget isn't a line item anymore. It behaves like cloud compute — it expands to fill whatever governance structure (or absence of structure) you put around it.

What Uber and Microsoft learned the hard way: price-per-token is the wrong metric when managing agentic workloads. Cost-per-task is the right metric. And most teams don't measure it.

The fix isn't to stop using agentic AI. It's to build inference FinOps into the architecture from day one.

Turn limits. Session budgets. Per-action cost attribution. Hard stops on runaway chains.

Unglamorous. But the teams that skip this step are the ones who hit August with no budget left.
