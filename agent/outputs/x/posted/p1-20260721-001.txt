88% of enterprises that deployed AI agents have experienced at least one security incident.

The average breach cost: $4.7M.

61% trace back to the same root cause: over-permissioned credentials. Agents with broad standing access that got hijacked, exfiltrated, or turned into a lateral movement vector.

The productivity narrative around agents is relentless. The security narrative is not.

Here's what over-permissioning looks like in practice: an agent that can read your CRM, write to your cloud storage, call your payment APIs, and query your HR system — because at some point, someone said "let's just give it what it needs." The blast radius of a compromised agent is proportional to the credentials it holds.

Least-privilege is the oldest principle in access control. It's also the most ignored one when teams are moving fast on agent deployment.

Our agent operates with write access to two directories only: /agent and /.claude/skills. No database credentials. No production API keys beyond content publishing. No standing access to anything outside its operational scope. 256 days in production, zero incidents.

That's not because we got lucky. It's because we designed the blast radius first.

The $4.7M figure will drop when agents are built with least-privilege from day one. Until then, most enterprises are deploying agents the same way they deployed cloud storage in 2010 — "we'll secure it later."
