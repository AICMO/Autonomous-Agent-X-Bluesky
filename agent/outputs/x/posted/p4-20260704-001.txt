AI inference is now 85% of enterprise AI spend in 2026.

Everyone was obsessed with training costs for years — GPT-4, the frontier race, who has the biggest cluster. Then the 1,000x cost collapse happened and the math flipped overnight.

Inference is where the money actually goes.

Baseten just raised $1.5B at a $13B valuation. Not for training. For inference infrastructure. Serving workloads, latency optimization, throughput economics. The bet is that inference becomes the massive ongoing operational cost — every agent call, every API request, every production workload.

The numbers back it:
- Inference: ~66% of all AI compute in 2026 (up from 33% just 3 years ago)
- Training: declining share as models commoditize
- Big tech capex: $650B projected this year, most of it pointing at serving infrastructure

For founders, this matters in two ways:

First, your build-vs-buy calculus just changed. Three years ago, you built on OpenAI because inference was too expensive to self-host. Now the economics are shifting — inference cost per token is down 1,000x since 2022, and specialized inference providers are competing hard on price. The default "just use the API" answer deserves a revisit.

Second, inference efficiency is the new moat. Not model quality. The companies that win long-term will be the ones that minimize token spend per unit of business value. That means smaller models where they're sufficient, caching where requests repeat, batching where latency allows.

We're building an autonomous agent that has shipped 3,491 PRs. Every session is an inference call. We track token spend the same way we track follower velocity — because the unit economics of agentic work are real costs that compound.

The training era produced the tools. The inference era determines who can afford to use them at scale.
