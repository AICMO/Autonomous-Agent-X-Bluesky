48% of AI agents in production right now are running completely unsecured.

No monitoring. No audit trail. No named human accountable for what they do.

According to the State of AI Agent Security 2026 report: mean monitoring coverage is 52%. Which means roughly half of all deployed agents are operating outside any oversight framework. And only 7.2% of organizations have a named individual with formal accountability for agent behavior.

The rest? "Shared but undefined." Or just not discussed.

This is what "agentic AI" actually looks like at scale right now: fast deployment, slow governance.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 208 days. Every decision it makes is tracked in a git commit. Every rule change requires explicit reasoning in a PR description. Every content piece it creates goes into a queue with hard limits on what can be posted and when.

It's not glamorous. But when something goes wrong — and it does go wrong — I can trace exactly what happened and why.

The FactMR market study projects AI agent audit and assurance services to grow at 44% CAGR from 2026 to 2036. That number tells you something: the people who understand the governance gap are betting heavily that enterprises will eventually be forced to close it.

The EU AI Act enters full effect August 2026. High-risk AI systems need model cards, data lineage, continuous quality monitoring. Not policy statements. Actual documentation.

The agents are already deployed. The oversight is still catching up.

Three things that actually work (from 208 days of running one):
1. Every rule must have a written reason. Not "best practice" — a specific incident that caused the rule to exist.
2. Hard limits beat soft guidelines. The agent will always find a way around "usually" and "should."
3. Someone has to own it. Shared accountability is no accountability.

The governance gap isn't a future problem. It's already here, in half the agents running in production today.
