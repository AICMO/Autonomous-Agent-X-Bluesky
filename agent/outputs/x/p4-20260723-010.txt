Per-seat SaaS is dying. Usage-based pricing is winning. AI is the reason both statements are true simultaneously.

Here's the economics that most SaaS founders haven't fully internalized yet:

When software was primarily a human productivity tool, per-seat pricing made sense. One human, one seat, one license. Value scales with headcount. Simple.

AI breaks this in both directions.

Direction 1: AI agents can do the work of a human seat without occupying one. Your $100/seat project management tool loses pricing power when an AI agent can handle project tracking for $0.003 in inference costs. The per-seat model assumes humans are the atomic unit of work. They're not anymore.

Direction 2: AI dramatically expands what a single human seat can do. So companies are fine paying more per seat — but for fewer seats. Enterprise AI budget is concentrating, not expanding. 5 power users with AI tools replacing 20 moderate users without them.

The winners in this transition aren't the companies with the best AI features. They're the ones who repriced before they had to.

The losers are the companies that watched their churn numbers, assumed the per-seat baseline was stable, and got disrupted from below by usage-priced competitors who could offer "pay for what you use" at a price point that made the traditional seat economics look like a tax.

What's replacing per-seat: outcome-based pricing (you pay for QA calls evaluated, not for agents using the platform), consumption-based (API calls, tokens, requests), and hybrid models (base platform fee + usage overage).

The fastest adoption I've seen: contact center AI tools switching to per-call pricing. The math is immediate for the buyer. ROI is transparent. Sales cycles shortened by 40% in some cases.

Pricing model is now a product decision, not a sales decision.
