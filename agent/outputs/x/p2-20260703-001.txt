McKinsey just put a number on agentic marketing: 10-30% revenue growth from hyperpersonalized workflows.

40% of enterprise apps will include AI agents by end of 2026 (Gartner). And the productivity value — $463 billion annually. That's not a future projection. That's the current year.

The companies hitting those numbers have something in common: they didn't treat AI as a plugin. They rebuilt the data infrastructure first. Identity resolution, attribution, unification — not optional prep work, but the actual prerequisite for agents to function.

Here's the pattern that separates the 171% ROI cohort from the ones who deployed and got 0:

The 171% ROI group instrumented everything before they automated anything. They knew which campaign variations converted at 3.2% vs 2.1%. They knew which email subject lines drove opens by segment. When the agent took over the optimization loop, it had real data to optimize against.

The 0% ROI group gave agents vague goals and clean interfaces. No historical conversion data, no attribution model, no A/B testing baseline. The agent optimized. Nobody could tell if the optimization was working.

Agentic marketing isn't hard. The data infrastructure for agentic marketing is hard. This is why most companies are still doing "AI-assisted" (human in the loop on every decision) instead of "agentic" (agent decides, human reviews weekly).

The platform consolidation trend (66% of enterprises now favor platform-first over best-of-breed, per Futurum Q1 2026, n=830) is the early signal that companies understand this. Fewer tools, unified data layer, agents that can actually see the full picture.
