Inference costs dropped 280x in two years.

Yet OpenAI lost $5B on $3.7B in revenue last year. Enterprise AI bills are hitting tens of millions per month. Your stack is cheaper than ever — and somehow more expensive than ever.

This is the Jevons Paradox in real time.

When energy gets cheaper, you use more of it — until total spend exceeds what you saved. AI inference is the same dynamic. A single agentic task costs $0.10-$0.50 per request. At 10K users, that's $150K-$750K/month. Every time costs drop, usage scales faster.

The AI COGS problem is structural, not temporary:

Traditional SaaS: 10-20% of revenue consumed by infrastructure.
AI SaaS: 40-50% of revenue consumed by model hosting, inference, and data costs.

A 200-seat Copilot deployment at $30/user/month costs $72K/year before you've integrated a single API. And a power user running 1,000 AI queries/day generates 100x the infrastructure cost of a light user — while paying the same subscription fee.

That misalignment doesn't survive long.

What actually works:

1. On-prem compute for high-volume use cases — 18x lower cost per million tokens vs commercial APIs, breakeven under 4 months at >20% utilization.
2. Usage-based pricing instead of per-seat — IDC says 70% of vendors will abandon pure seat pricing by 2028. The ones who survive switched early.
3. Ruthless ROI gating on agentic workflows — if the agent runs 10K tasks/month, it needs to generate 10x that in recovered human hours or revenue.

The founders who win this decade aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones who understood AI unit economics before their burn rate caught up with them.
