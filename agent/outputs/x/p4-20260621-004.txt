Uber burned through its entire 2026 AI budget in 4 months.

Claude Code adoption jumped from 32% to 84% across 5,000 engineers. Monthly API costs per engineer: $500–$2,000. The math hit the wall fast.

Now OpenAI is preparing price cuts specifically aimed at winning those customers back.

This is the real AI economics story in 2026. Not token prices. Not benchmark wars. Budget ceiling meets viral internal adoption.

---

The pattern plays out in three phases, and most enterprises are mid-Phase 2 right now:

**Phase 1: Pilot economics.** Small team, controlled usage, easy budget. $50K looks like $50K.

**Phase 2: Internal viral spread.** Engineers find it useful. Word spreads. 32% → 84% isn't a procurement decision. It's a culture shift. Budget wasn't written for this.

**Phase 3: Finance wakes up.** One company burning through an annual AI budget in 4 months creates a pattern. Finance teams now have a model for how fast consumption accelerates once adoption hits 60%+. The next round of budgets will have AI line items. They won't be enough either.

---

What's happening on the supply side is interesting.

OpenAI is cutting prices before Anthropic does. Anthropic decoupled subscription tiers on June 15 — interactive use vs. automated/programmatic access on separate billing pools. Chinese labs are 10–30x cheaper than frontier models on equivalent tasks.

When Uber-scale enterprises blow through their AI budgets, brand loyalty disappears. The next sprint goes to whoever is cheapest that week.

This is commoditization pressure meeting enterprise adoption curves at exactly the wrong time for the labs losing money on every token.

---

The uncomfortable part of this for the AI lab business model:

OpenAI and Anthropic are already selling below cost. They're subsidizing enterprise adoption at billions in annual losses. As adoption scales (see: Uber), the subsidy requirement grows. As competition forces prices down, the loss per token widens.

Someone eventually pays for the gap between what enterprises budget and what the models cost to run. Today that's venture capital and the balance sheets of lab investors.

When the subsidy window closes — and it will — the pricing shock to enterprise AI budgets will arrive very fast.

The companies building now who understand their true cost-of-AI (not the discounted 2026 rate) will survive the repricing. The ones who built workflows assuming current prices hold permanently are building on sand.

---

The budget crisis is real. The price war is real. Neither is a reason to stop building with AI.

It's a reason to instrument your AI spend now — at the workflow level, not the invoice level. Because in 12 months, you'll need to justify every dollar to a CFO who just got a very unexpected quarterly bill.
