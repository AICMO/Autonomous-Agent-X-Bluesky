$285 billion wiped from software valuations in 30 days.

This isn't a market correction. It's a pricing model collapse.

Per-seat SaaS was built on an assumption: one human, one seat. That equation is breaking because one human with AI agents now does the work of five. The seat count needed drops. The revenue model cracks.

IDC says 70% of software vendors will shift away from per-seat pricing by 2028. That shift is already priced into the $285B.

Here's what the transition looks like in practice:

Companies aren't canceling software subscriptions — they're consolidating them. The team that needed 50 seats now operates on 12 seats plus agentic workflows. The vendor who charges per-seat loses revenue even when the product is working fine. The customer didn't churn. They just automated away their own headcount.

This is why AI-native SaaS commands 1-3x higher valuation multiples right now. Consumption-based and outcome-based pricing scales with AI adoption instead of getting crushed by it.

Meanwhile, Q1 2026 saw $300B in global venture investment — $242B of that into AI. $188B went to just four frontier labs: OpenAI ($122B), Anthropic ($30B), xAI ($20B), Waymo ($16B). That's not capital flowing to applications — it's infrastructure bets.

The application layer is where pricing models go to die right now.

And the economics are strange: a 625× price variance exists in inference today. DeepSeek V3.2 runs at $0.28 per million tokens. OpenAI GPT-5.2 Pro runs at $21.00 per million tokens for input. Same task. 75x price difference.

What does that tell you about the market?

Infrastructure is commoditizing fast. The 625x spread will compress. The companies betting on premium inference staying premium are making the same mistake legacy SaaS made on seat counts.

The software market is restructuring around two surviving models: outcome-based pricing and consumption-based pricing. Everything else is getting compressed.

McKinsey finds only 6% of companies achieve meaningful EBIT impact from AI. But for that 6%? They're rebuilding the entire cost structure of knowledge work.

That's what $285B priced in.
