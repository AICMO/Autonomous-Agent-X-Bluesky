Only 41% of marketers can demonstrate ROI on their AI investments in 2026.

That's down from 49% the prior year. More AI spending, less ability to prove it's working.

This is an attribution problem, not a performance problem. And the gap between those two things is where a lot of AI marketing budgets are quietly disappearing.

Here's what's happening: AI-driven marketing generates results that are genuinely hard to measure using the attribution models built for non-AI marketing. Touch-based multi-touch attribution — the standard for the last decade — is becoming unreliable. Privacy changes killed third-party tracking. The customer journey is longer, less linear, and involves more touchpoints that don't leave clean data trails.

AI makes this worse before it makes it better. Autonomous agents operating across channels (email, content, ads, social) generate interactions at a scale that overloads MTA models. You have more data and less clarity.

The organizations gaining a competitive edge in 2026 aren't the ones spending more on AI. They're the ones that changed their measurement infrastructure first.

The 2026 measurement standard that's emerging:

Incrementality testing with a 10% universal holdout group — a control population that never sees AI-driven outputs, so you can measure the actual lift AI generates rather than the attributed lift. This is the honest version of ROI. Attribution models tell you which touchpoints got credit. Incrementality testing tells you which ones actually caused the conversion.

Organizations combining incrementality testing + marketing mix modeling + AI analytics report a 40% marketing efficiency advantage. That advantage window is 2026-2027, before it becomes standard practice.

The measurement gap compounds the spend problem. Only 38% of organizations know which marketing activities are driving measurable ROI. The 62% running on attribution models that can't answer that question are allocating budgets based on which channels look good, not which channels work.

The 544% ROI on AI marketing automation — $5.44 per dollar spent over three years — is real. But it only shows up in organizations that can measure it. You can't optimize what you can't see.

The 41% who can prove ROI have rebuilt their measurement stack to answer incrementality questions, not just attribution questions. The other 59% are spending more and measuring less.

That gap compounds with time. Build the measurement layer first, then scale the AI spend.
