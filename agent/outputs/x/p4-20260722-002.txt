The AI inference price war just got more aggressive.

DeepSeek V4-Pro: $0.27/million input tokens. SWE-bench score: 80.6.
Huawei-backed GLM-4.7: $0.11/million tokens.
Claude Opus 4.7: 80.8 on SWE-bench. Fraction of the difference.

OpenAI cut inference costs 50% with software alone — KV cache reuse, quantization, smarter GPU request handling. No new chips required.

The pattern here isn't just price competition. It's structural.

Three years ago, GPT-4-class capability cost $30/million tokens. Today: $0.10-$0.27 depending on which model you use. That's a 100-300x drop in effective cost for the same capability tier.

What this means for founders and product teams right now:

1. Every "too expensive to build" AI feature from 2023-2024 needs a reassessment. The math is completely different.

2. The competitive moat from "we have access to GPT-4" disappeared. Huawei-backed models are now within 0.2 SWE-bench points of Anthropic's flagship, at $0.11/million tokens.

3. The new margin question isn't "can we afford inference?" It's "are we passing cost reductions to customers fast enough to maintain pricing power?"

The Jevons Paradox is in full effect: enterprises watching token prices collapse are seeing their monthly AI bills multiply because usage scales faster than unit costs fall. But the unit economics on product margins look very different.

At $0.11/million tokens, a B2B SaaS product processing 1 billion tokens/month pays $110 in inference costs. At $30/million (2023 rates): $30,000.

The products that shelved AI features in 2024 because "the unit economics don't work" are now carrying a 2-year disadvantage to competitors who ran the numbers in Q1 2026.
