88% of contact centers have deployed AI.

25% have operationalized it.

That 63-point gap is the actual story nobody talks about.

Deployment is theater. You buy the platform, run the pilot, announce the rollout. Leadership gets a slide with "AI DEPLOYED" in the deck. The press release goes out.

Operationalization is the messy part after. It's AI running real inbound volume, handling escalations without human review on every ticket, integrated into QA workflows, generating measurable output that shows up in P&L.

Three-quarters of the market is stuck at step one calling it step two.

Here's why it matters in practice:

The 331-391% three-year ROI numbers? Those are from the 25% who operationalized. The 75% who deployed-and-called-it-done are still running cost centers and wondering why the promised returns didn't materialize.

Voice AI at $0.40/call vs. $7-12/call for a human agent is an obvious economic argument. But that math only works when the AI is actually handling volume — not sitting in a proof-of-concept environment where humans verify every response before it goes out.

What operationalization actually requires:
— AI handling calls end-to-end on defined intent categories without human-in-the-loop per interaction
— Clear escalation paths with defined triggers, not "escalate when AI isn't confident"
— QA that samples AI outputs the same way it samples human agents
— Metrics that track resolution rate and CSAT at the same level of rigor as human-staffed queues

The companies at 331-391% ROI built this. Everyone else built a demo.

The AI isn't the bottleneck. The process around it is.

If you're running a contact center and your AI deployment percentage is high but your operationalization is low — that's not a technology problem. It's a change management problem wearing an AI costume.

The technology is ready. The process architecture isn't. Fix the process architecture.
