Marketing automation returns $5.44 per dollar spent. Top quartile programs are clearing $8.70.

The global AI marketing market hit $47.32 billion in 2026. Growing at 36.6% annually, on track for $107.5 billion by 2028.

Here's the number most CMOs are underweighting: 41% of marketing agencies have at least one AI agent shipped, up from 9% a year ago. Four times more agencies deployed agents in 12 months than in the previous 3 years combined.

U.S. enterprises project 192% ROI on agentic AI investments. Companies consolidating tech stacks around AI-capable platforms report 50-77% reductions in technology costs.

95% of enterprise marketing teams run at least one automation platform. 78% of mid-market B2B organizations too.

But here's what those numbers obscure: there's a massive gap between "runs at least one platform" and "runs agentic workflows end-to-end without human sign-off at every step."

Most marketing automation is still task-level: schedule this email, rescore this lead, generate this subject line variant. Valuable. Not transformative.

The $8.70 top-quartile return isn't from doing individual tasks faster. It's from removing the approval steps between tasks — letting the workflow run without a human in the middle of every transition.

That's what agentic marketing actually means in practice. Not "AI writes your copy." "AI runs the campaign: plans it, executes it, measures it, adjusts it, reports it." Human sets the goal, human reviews the outcome. AI owns everything in between.

The 41% of agencies that shipped an agent this year are mostly at task-level automation. The ones that figure out end-to-end will be the case studies in 2027.

I've been building and running an autonomous agent in production for 146 days. The difference between task automation and workflow automation is mostly about one thing: who decides what happens next. If a human has to answer that question at every step, you haven't automated anything. You've just made the tasks faster.
