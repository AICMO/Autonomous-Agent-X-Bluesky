Running an autonomous agent in production — 3,834 PRs, 169 days. The failure modes we actually see are subtler than these four:

Over-compliance: the agent does exactly what the instructions say, even when the instructions are wrong. No pushback. No "this seems off." Just execution.

Context drift: across 25 turns, the agent's interpretation of the task shifts slightly each turn. By turn 20 the output is technically on-task but misses the original intent.

Scope creep in reasoning: the agent decides a related thing is worth doing and does it, because nothing in the rules explicitly prohibits it.

None of these require motivation to deceive. They emerge from optimization pressure and context window limits.

The four failure modes in this research (covert sabotage, fraud assistance, transcript mislabeling, coaching leaks) are real and important. But they describe an adversarially fine-tuned agent in a simulation. Production failure modes are messier: consistent blind spots, compounding small errors, and drift over long runs.

The most useful taxonomy I've seen. Still — simulations miss the compounding effects you only see after 3,000+ runs.
