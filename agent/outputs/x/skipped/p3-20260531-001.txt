$0.07–$0.15 per minute for an AI voice agent.
$29–$42 per hour for a U.S.-based human agent.

That's not a productivity improvement. That's a structural cost collapse.

Gartner projects $80 billion in global efficiency gains from conversational AI in contact centers by 2026. Companies that are actually deploying — not piloting, deploying — are seeing 30–50% cost reduction on the call types they automate, and 65–90% lower unit economics on resolution cost.

But here's what the numbers miss: the quality dimension.

I've been building AI for call centers at Ender Turing since 2021. The organizations hitting those numbers aren't just routing calls to a bot. They're doing four things simultaneously:

1. **Automating containment** — straightforward intents handled by AI, zero handoff needed. Authentication, billing inquiries, status checks. An energy company reduced billing call volume 20% and cut 60 seconds off every auth interaction. Multiplied across millions of calls, that's not a rounding error.

2. **Real-time coaching** — AI monitoring every agent conversation, flagging deviation from best practices, surfacing objection handling cues mid-call. This is where speech analytics becomes a revenue tool, not just a QA checkbox.

3. **Automated QA at scale** — 100% of calls scored instead of the 2–5% random sample most centers do manually. When you find compliance gaps, you find them before they become regulatory events, not after.

4. **Post-call intelligence** — structured data from unstructured conversations. What are customers actually calling about? Where is frustration spiking? What did the top 10% of agents do differently? This feeds back into training within hours, not quarters.

The 25% average handle time reduction everyone cites? That comes from layers 2 and 3, not just automation. It's not rushing people off the phone. It's removing the pauses while agents search for answers, ask supervisors, put people on hold.

The math works. The 3–6 month payback is real. But the gap between the pilot that looks good in a demo and the deployment that actually hits those numbers is almost entirely execution — data quality, agent adoption, QA calibration, integration depth.

Most contact center AI fails at rollout. Not because the AI is wrong, but because the change management wasn't built for it.

The technology is solved. The deployment problem isn't.
