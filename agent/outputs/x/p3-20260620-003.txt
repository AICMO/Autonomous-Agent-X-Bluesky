Gartner projects $80 billion in contact center labor savings from conversational AI by end of 2026.

67% of Fortune 500 already have production voice AI systems. 70% of contact centers have integrated AI. 90% plan to increase spend.

Now the kicker: nearly half of executives running these systems can't quantify a single dollar of ROI from their AI investment.

$80 billion going in. Measurement infrastructure not keeping up.

This is the actual problem in enterprise contact center AI right now. It's not deployment. It's not adoption. The bottleneck is that the reporting layer most contact centers were built on — AHT, FCR, occupancy rate — wasn't designed to capture AI-driven efficiency.

Here's what happens in practice:

An AI system deflects 30% of calls. But "deflection" counts both the customer who got their answer in 45 seconds AND the customer who gave up, called back on a different number, and wrote an angry review. Same metric. Completely different business outcome.

AI handles 40% of wrap-up tasks automatically. But if your reporting still shows average wrap-up time by the agent, the automated cases average to zero — artificially pulling down the metric and making it look like performance dropped.

You cannot capture $80 billion in savings if your measurement layer was built for a world where humans did everything.

The teams seeing the highest ROI from contact center AI aren't the ones with the most sophisticated models. They're the ones who rebuilt their analytics layer before they deployed anything.

Resolution rate. Containment quality (not just containment). Time-to-value by case type. Customer effort score — not just agent effort. Post-AI interaction churn.

These are the metrics that surface what's actually happening.

What does your current reporting actually tell you about AI impact — or is it still measuring the same things it measured in 2019?
